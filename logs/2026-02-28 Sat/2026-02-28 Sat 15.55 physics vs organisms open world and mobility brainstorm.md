## The Dial: Physics vs. Organisms

This is the core design question. There's a spectrum:

```
[Full physics]  ←——————————————————→  [Full evolution]
Define Physarum's       Define only conservation
mechanics in code.      laws; evolve everything.
No evolved behavior     No predictable behavior
is possible — you       is possible — you
wrote the answer.       generate noise.
```

**If you specify too much physics**, you're writing the organism, not evolving it. The experiment becomes a validation that your simulation is correct, not a discovery of emergent strategy. That's interesting but limited — it's more of an agent-based model than experimental evolution.

**If you specify too little**, evolution has too much freedom. The search space is intractable. You get either trivial solutions (fixed point attractors) or incomprehensible ones. No mechanism for analysis.

**The useful middle ground**: physics defines *what is possible and what is costly*, not *what is optimal*. You set the constraint set and the cost structure. Evolution finds strategies that exploit those constraints efficiently.

For Physarum specifically, this means:
- **Physics defines**: diffusion of nutrients, that infra costs maintenance, that energy can flow between connected cells, that infra can be built/dissolved
- **Organisms evolve**: *when* and *how much* to build in which direction, *when* to pull back vs. extend, *how* to route internal transport

The litmus test: if the right physics setting produces Physarum-like behavior even with a *random* (non-evolved) network, the physics is overdetermined and you're simulating, not evolving. If organisms with a specific network topology outcompete those without it, the topology is genuinely evolved.

**Practically**: start minimal. Add physics only when absence of a mechanism is the specific reason you can't see the target behavior.

## What Environment Did Physarum Actually Evolve In?

Forest floors — specifically: decaying wood, leaf litter, bark, soil. The relevant features:

- **Discrete, clustered food patches**: bacterial colonies, fungal mats, spores, decaying organic matter. Not uniform — highly patchy and spatially structured.
- **Mobile organisms as competitors and food**: bacteria swim. Nematodes crawl. Insect larvae move. Physarum evolved in an environment where some of its food *moved* and some of its competitors *moved*. The current model has none of this.
- **Variable moisture**: Physarum dries out and enters a dormant (sclerotium) state in dry conditions, resumes when moist. Moisture is a major environmental gradient.
- **Temperature gradients**: Physarum avoids light (phototaxis) and prefers cooler, darker regions.
- **Scale**: Physarum can cover square meters. Its "decision making" (shortest path finding) operates on that scale. Individual cells are microns. The relevant scale is many orders of magnitude larger than a single cell.

**The mobile organisms point is critical.** Physarum's environment includes both sessile resources (decaying wood, bacterial patches anchored to surfaces) *and* mobile ones. The flagellated bacteria that Physarum eats aren't just sitting there — they're swimming around, potentially escaping, potentially clustering. This creates a fundamentally different selective pressure than competing only with other sessile organisms.

In the current model, everything is sessile by construction. There's no concept of a particle that moves independently of the grid. This isn't necessarily wrong — it's a simplification — but it means the model misses the predator/prey dynamics that may have shaped Physarum's behavior.

## Sessile Organisms in Experimental Evolution — Are They Interesting?

Yes, enormously. Many of the most interesting questions in biology involve sessile organisms:

- **Biofilms**: bacteria anchor to surfaces, form structured communities with division of labor (some produce protective matrix, some specialize in nutrient uptake, some form dispersal cells). Sessile by design.
- **Coral reefs**: the most biodiverse marine ecosystems. Every coral polyp is sessile. Competition is purely spatial — growth, overgrowth, chemical warfare, symbiosis.
- **Plant root systems**: explore soil for water and nutrients through tip growth. Compete with neighbors without ever moving.
- **Fungal networks** (mycorrhizae): the largest organisms on Earth are fungal mats. Completely sessile. Highly structured, efficient transport networks.
- **Stromatolites**: 3.5 billion year old microbial mats. Sessile. Layered. Among the earliest complex living structures.

The key insight: **sessile organisms compete through *growth strategy* rather than *locomotion strategy*.** This is potentially a *cleaner* experimental setup for studying intelligence because:
- The organism's "decisions" are about resource allocation, not motor control
- Spatial patterns are directly observable and interpretable
- Competition outcomes are determined by cumulative growth strategy, not reflexes

The current CoralAI model is in this tradition. The question is whether the physics is rich enough that interesting growth strategies can emerge.

## "Mobile Particles are Essential" — Is This True?

This claim is worth examining. Mobile particles would add:
1. **Predator/prey dynamics** — things can eat each other by moving
2. **Dispersal** — propagules (spores, seeds) that travel and found new colonies
3. **Environmental heterogeneity** — a passing raindrop, a crawling beetle reshaping the landscape

But mobile particles are expensive:
- Computationally: tracking individual particle positions and collisions
- Analytically: hard to interpret behavior when particles are everywhere
- Architecturally: requires a fundamentally different data structure (particle list vs. grid field)

**Alternative**: many of these can be approximated without true particle motion:
- Dispersal: stochastic seeding of new organisms at random distant locations (radiation events already do this)
- Environmental perturbation: stochastic patch resets, occasional grid-wide events (see below)
- Predation: an organism can "consume" a neighboring cell's infra (effectively eating it) — this requires no mobile particles, just a new physics rule

True mobility is probably worth adding eventually but is not necessary for a large class of interesting experiments. The "forest floor" environmental model (see below) can compensate by making the *environment* dynamic even when organisms are sessile.

## Petri Dish vs. Forest Floor — Open vs. Closed Systems

**The petri dish problem**: it's closed. Nutrients deplete, organisms saturate, equilibrium is reached. Long-run dynamics are boring — everything dies or everything maxes out.

**The forest floor alternative**: open system sustained by continuous external energy input (sunlight → photosynthesis → organic matter → soil nutrients). Runs indefinitely because input roughly balances loss.

For a computational model, "open system" means the environment has its own dynamics that don't depend on the organisms. Some mechanisms:

### Continuous input
- **Slow nutrient regeneration**: patches regrow at a rate independent of organisms. This is the simplest version — patches are sinks of organism energy and sources of their own energy over time.
- **Periodic nutrient pulse**: every N steps, randomly seed M new patches. Represents new organic matter falling.

### Stochastic disturbance
- **Falling tree**: randomly clear a large patch (radius 10-30 cells) of all infra + organisms. Survivors at the margins now have open territory and released nutrients.
- **Rain/flood**: diffuse all nutrient channels by a large factor in one step. Redistributes concentrated patches across a wider area. Could also wash away some energy/infra.
- **Fire/drought**: globally reduce all energy by a factor (starvation event). Only organisms with large infra reserves survive. Creates periodic mass extinction with subsequent radiation.
- **Seasonal cycle**: sinusoidal modulation of baseline nutrient injection rate. Winter = near-zero input, summer = high input.

### Geological time
- **Slow landscape change**: the infra channel of the environment (if distinguished from organism infra) shifts over thousands of steps. Gradients that were favorable become unfavorable.

These mechanisms keep the system far from equilibrium indefinitely, which is necessary for sustained open-ended evolution. Without them, evolution terminates at a locally stable state.

**Key point**: you don't need to simulate all of these at once. Even just "periodic nutrient pulse + rare disturbance event" dramatically changes the long-run behavior relative to the current static source field.

## Competing Infrastructure as a Food Source

This is an underappreciated point. In the current model, when an organism is displaced (genome overwritten), its infra remains on the grid. This infra is a resource waiting to be harvested — but there's no mechanism to harvest it except by colonizing that cell and then liquidating the infra.

**What this enables (if implemented)**:
- **Decomposers**: organisms that specialize in colonizing dead territory and extracting its stored infra energy. They don't compete for live territory — they follow behind competition and clean up.
- **Saprophytes**: biological analogy to decomposing fungi. Break down dead organic matter. Crucial ecological recycler.
- **Recycling loop**: organisms build infra, die (or are displaced), infra slowly degrades to nutrient, nutrient feeds next generation. The environment "remembers" where life was — there's ecological history baked into the landscape.

This is actually already partially present in the current model — dead cells' infra persists. What's missing is:
1. A mechanism for infra to slowly convert to nutrient (infra decay → nutrient release, not just loss)
2. The ability to actively liquidate a *neighbor's* infra (consume it rather than just inherit it)

Point 2 would immediately create predator/prey-like dynamics without any mobile particles — organisms can "eat" the built structure of their neighbors.

## Criticality: Solid/Liquid/Gas Analogy

The energy-infra-nutrient triad maps cleanly onto thermodynamic phases:

| Phase | Analogy | Properties |
|-------|---------|------------|
| Gas/dissolved | Nutrient in medium | Diffuses freely, not owned, available to all |
| Liquid | Free energy | Mobile, held but not fixed, can flow |
| Solid | Infrastructure | Fixed in space, persistent, ordered, costly to change |

Phase transitions:
- Nutrient → Energy: **digestion/metabolism** (contact required, organism-controlled rate)
- Energy → Infra: **investment** (organism choice, irreversible in short term)
- Infra → Energy: **liquidation** (organism choice, destroys structure)
- Infra → Nutrient: **decay/decomposition** (physics, slow, releases locked resources)

Biological systems operate near the **liquid-solid boundary** (criticality) — partially ordered, partially fluid. The reason: at criticality, small inputs cause large, correlated responses. This is believed to be why neurons operate near criticality (maximum dynamic range and information processing).

For CoralAI: the ratio of energy to infra in the global system could be tracked as a criticality measure. If the system is "too solid" (all infra, no free energy), it can't respond to disturbances. If "too liquid" (all energy, no infra), it can't maintain structure. Evolution might naturally push toward the boundary.

This could even be a measurable output: track E/(E+I) over time and see whether it converges to a stable intermediate value under different physics.

## Bidirectional Transport / Peristalsis

**The problem with passive transport**: in the current model, energy flows toward high-infra neighbors. This is passive — determined by the infra landscape. The organism has no control over flow direction. This creates several problems:

1. Energy accumulates at infra peaks (the cluster), starving the frontier
2. The organism can't actively send energy toward the frontier to enable expansion
3. Peristalsis (bidirectional, phase-propagating flow) is impossible — you need directional control

**Physarum's solution**: peristaltic pumping. Each tube segment has contractile proteins. The contraction wave propagates along the network with a phase delay, creating bulk flow. The direction and speed of the wave determines flow direction. Crucially, the organism can reverse flow direction by reversing the wave propagation pattern.

**In the model**: this could be implemented by giving the neural network a "pump" output — for each neighbor, a signed value:
- Positive: push energy toward that neighbor (outward flow, toward frontier)
- Negative: pull energy from that neighbor (inward flow, toward cluster)

The physics of pumping: `energy_transferred = pump_strength * infra_fraction * energy_available`

With this:
- Organisms can route energy through their network actively, not just passively
- A connected organism could evolve to send energy outward during expansion and inward during consolidation
- Peristaltic-like behavior (wave of pump activations) could emerge from evolved temporal patterns in the network

**Why organism-controlled transport is important**: the organism needs to solve a supply chain problem. The cluster has energy (from nutrient digestion) but needs to send it to the frontier (for expansion). The frontier has new territory but needs energy to consolidate. Passive flow (toward infra peaks) always moves energy the wrong way — toward the cluster, not the frontier. Active pumping is the only solution.

This is probably the single most important physics addition for enabling Physarum-like network behavior.

## Signaling Halos Around Food Sources

Physarum detects food through chemical gradients — food releases attractant molecules that diffuse through the substrate faster than the food itself moves. Physarum grows pseudopodia preferentially toward higher attractant concentration.

**The problem with nutrients as the only signal**: nutrients diffuse slowly (D~0.005 cells²/step → spreads ~1 cell per ~200 steps). By the time a nutrient gradient reaches an organism 10 cells away, ~2000 steps have passed. Effectively undetectable at distance.

**Solution: separate signal channel with higher diffusivity**

```
nutrient  — actual food; diffuses slowly (D=0.005), depleted on contact
signal    — chemical attractant; emitted by nutrient patches, diffuses fast (D=0.1), decays fast
```

Each step:
- `signal += nutrient * emission_rate`
- `signal = diffuse(signal, D=0.1)`
- `signal *= (1 - decay_rate)` (e.g., 0.05 per step → signal halves every ~14 steps)

With D=0.1, signal spreads ~3 cells per step. A patch 10 cells away produces detectable signal within ~35 steps — feasible. The signal decays fast enough that it points toward the source (gradient is steep) rather than saturating the whole grid.

Organisms that evolve to sense the signal channel can perform chemotaxis — growing preferentially toward food. This doesn't need to be designed into the physics; it emerges when:
1. Signal channel is included in neural network inputs
2. There's a survival benefit to finding food (nutrient depletion means only those near patches thrive)

**Additional signal types** that could be modeled the same way:
- **Pheromone/communication**: organisms emit a signal that other organisms (or their own distant cells) can detect. Enables coordination.
- **Alarm signal**: cells under attack emit a signal that neighbors respond to defensively.
- **Quorum sensing**: organisms emit a signal proportional to their density. High density triggers different behavior (biofilm formation, spore production).

## "Each Run Is The Same" — Why and What To Do

This is a serious problem for open-ended evolution. If every run converges to the same qualitative behavior, the system isn't exploring its strategy space. Causes:

1. **No resource heterogeneity**: uniform injection → uniform environment → uniform optimal strategy → convergent evolution to the same solution every time. Fix: spatial resource structure.

2. **No temporal dynamics**: static environment → no need to maintain behavioral diversity. A population that's well-adapted to a static environment will converge and stay there. Fix: disturbance events, seasonal cycles.

3. **Aggressive culling**: killing lowest-cell-count genomes when >100 genomes exist eliminates rare strategies before they can establish. Fix: fixed genome pool (already in known issues).

4. **Linear network**: the current LinearNet ignores hidden nodes — NEAT can evolve complex topologies but the forward pass is always linear input → output. This massively limits the strategy space. Fix: use a proper recurrent or feedforward NEAT network.

5. **No historical contingency**: evolution resets each run from the same initial state. Small differences in early random events should lead to different evolutionary trajectories (founder effects), but the current setup doesn't have enough of this. Fix: strongly stochastic initialization and environment.

The forest-floor model with periodic disturbance would directly address (2) and create (5). Spatial resources address (1). These two changes are probably most impactful.

## A Proposed Hierarchy of Experiments

From most constrained (most engineered) to least (most emergent):

### Level 1: Physics only, no evolution (current energy_dynamics experiment)
Define the environment richly. Does physics alone produce interesting structure?
- Add nutrient patches, signal diffusion, slow nutrient diffusion
- Verify that energy self-organizes into meaningful spatial patterns
- No organisms, no NEAT

### Level 2: Fixed organisms, evolving physics parameters
Hardcode simple strategies (e.g., always invest 50%, always explore north). Vary physics parameters. What physics settings produce stable ecosystems vs. extinction vs. monoculture?
- This characterizes the fitness landscape before adding evolution
- Can be done analytically (or near-analytically)

### Level 3: Evolving organisms, simple environment (current coral experiment, improved)
Fix the physics, let NEAT evolve strategies:
- Add spatial resources (nutrient patches)
- Add infra decay (maintenance cost)
- Fix softmax → tanh trade
- Fix culling → fixed genome pool
- See if diverse strategies emerge; see if each run is different

### Level 4: Evolving organisms, dynamic environment (forest floor)
Add temporal dynamics:
- Stochastic disturbance (clearing events, nutrient pulses)
- Seasonal cycles
- See if environmental diversity produces behavioral diversity
- Measure complexity over time

### Level 5: Organisms + organism-controlled transport
Add the pump action. See if:
- Transport networks emerge
- Peristaltic-like patterns arise
- Efficient supply chain behavior appears without engineering it

### Level 6: Multiple signaling channels, communication, decomposers
Add signal diffusion, alarm signals, inter-organism communication. Does anything resembling coordination emerge?
