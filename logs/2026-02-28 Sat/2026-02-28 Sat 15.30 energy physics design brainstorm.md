# Energy Physics Design Brainstorm

*2026-02-28 — design thinking*

---

## The Question

The current energy system has energy being injected uniformly (via a source field) and flowing toward high-infra neighbors. This is functional but feels disconnected from any physical or biological analogy. What *should* energy mean in a system like this? What should it do?

---

## What Energy Does in an Actual Petri Dish

### The medium itself
Agar is a gel. Nutrients (glucose, amino acids, minerals) are dissolved in it at some initial concentration. They diffuse through the gel — but slowly. Diffusion in agar is **Fickian**: each molecule does a random walk, spreading proportional to √t. For typical nutrients in agar, the diffusion coefficient is on the order of 10⁻⁹ m²/s. That's slow — a nutrient molecule spreads about 1 mm in an hour. On the cellular scale this is essentially local: bacteria experience a zone of depletion around themselves as they consume.

### Sources and sinks
- **Sources**: the agar itself (pre-loaded), occasional fresh medium additions, or secretions from other organisms
- **Sinks**: consumption by cells; no other loss (the dish is sealed)
- **There is no convection in agar** — no bulk flow. Every molecule moves by diffusion or by being actively transported.

### Active transport
Bacteria can swim (chemotaxis toward nutrients), but they don't pump nutrients through a network. **Slime molds do.** Physarum polycephalum builds tube networks and pumps cytoplasm through them via peristaltic contraction — oscillatory squeezing that drives bulk flow. This is fundamentally different from diffusion: it's active, directional, and scales with tube diameter.

### The contact requirement
A key fact: to metabolize a nutrient, a cell must be physically present at its location. You can't eat food from a distance. The nutrient has to contact the cell membrane, be taken in, and processed. This creates a **foraging incentive** — if food doesn't come to you, you must go to it.

## What Energy Means to a Slime Mold

Physarum is the clearest biological analogy to this system:

- **Food sources**: discrete patches of bacteria, fungal spores, dead organic matter
- **"Energy"**: cytoplasm / metabolized nutrients flowing through the network
- **"Infrastructure"**: the tube network (protoplasmic veins)

The tubes don't contain energy — they *transport* it. More tubes, or thicker tubes, means faster transport. Tubes cost something to maintain (you have to keep them pressurized, keep the walls intact). Unused tubes atrophy and dissolve — the slime mold withdraws resources from paths that aren't carrying flow.

**The positive feedback that builds network structure**: thick tubes → more flow → mechanical signal to thicken further. Tubes that happen to be on a high-flow path grow; tubes on low-flow paths shrink. This is how Physarum builds efficient networks without any central planning.

**The foraging dynamic**: when Physarum encounters food at a new location, it:
1. Engulfs the food (contact digestion)
2. Metabolizes it → free energy (cytoplasm)
3. Pumps that energy back through the network to the rest of the organism
4. Uses some of that energy to grow new tubes toward other potential food sources

So the flow direction is: **food patch → local digestion → energy → transport along infra → growth of new tubes → reach more food**.

This is a closed loop where the physical network is both the product of past foraging and the instrument of future foraging.

## What Energy vs Infrastructure Actually Means (Abstract)

The two-state model has a deep structure. Energy and infra are the same underlying resource in two different phases:

| Property | Energy | Infrastructure |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| Mobility | High — flows, diffuses | Zero — spatially fixed |
| Persistence | Low — gets used up | High — persists until decayed or liquidated |
| Utility | Immediate — can be spent on anything | Future — enables/amplifies future energy flow |
| Analogy (biological) | ATP, cytoplasm, free glucose | Biomass, membranes, enzyme networks |
| Analogy (economic) | Cash, liquid capital | Factories, roads, physical capital |
| Analogy (thermodynamic) | Free energy (Gibbs) | Bound energy (structure) |

The invest/liquidate cycle is a **phase transition** between these two states. This is why it matters:

- If you only have energy (no infra), it flows away — you can't hold territory or accumulate
- If you only have infra (no energy), you can't act — no currency to spend
- The interesting region is in between: organisms with enough infra to attract energy but enough free energy to expand

The reason they're separate isn't arbitrary — it's what creates the **growth/exploitation tradeoff**. Invest too much → you hold territory well but can't colonize new ground. Invest too little → energy leaks away to neighbors with better infra.

## What's Wrong with the Current Implementation

### Problem 1: Energy injection is spatially homogeneous

The source field provides uniform injection. Every cell gets the same energy input regardless of location. This means:

- **There's nothing to find.** The optimal strategy is pure territorial defense, not exploration.
- Navigation — the ability to find and exploit resource patches — provides zero benefit.
- All selective pressure is on *holding* territory, none on *finding* territory.

In biological systems, resources are always patchy. That patchiness is the *reason* organisms evolved navigation, chemotaxis, tube networks.

### Problem 2: Energy flows toward infra, creating pure positive feedback

Energy accumulates at infra peaks. More infra → more energy → more ability to invest → more infra. This is a winner-take-all dynamic. It will produce a landscape with a few very dominant cells and everyone else starved.

Real slime mold networks don't work this way. Energy flows *through* the network to reach food patches and distribute throughout the organism. High-infra regions are *conduits*, not sinks.

### Problem 3: No contact requirement for energy acquisition

Energy just appears everywhere from the source field. There's no reason to physically explore. A cell in an empty field gets the same input as a cell sitting on a nutrient patch.

## What the Physics Should Probably Look Like

### Two-layer resource model

Separate **environmental nutrients** (raw food) from **free energy** (metabolized):

```
nutrient  (ti.f32) — dissolved in the environment, diffuses slowly, depletes on contact
energy    (ti.f32) — metabolized, held by organisms, flows along infra network
infra     (ti.f32) — structural investment, enables energy transport, has maintenance cost
```

**Nutrient dynamics:**
- Initializes in discrete patches (corners, random blobs, gradient — whatever environment)
- Diffuses very slowly each step (D ~ 0.001–0.01 cells²/step)
- Depletes when a living cell (with infra) is present: `nutrient[i,j] -= metabolism_rate * infra[i,j]`
- Regenerates slowly (logistic growth toward carrying capacity) or periodically respawns patches

**Energy dynamics:**
- Gained by metabolizing local nutrients: `energy += metabolized_nutrient`
- Flows along the infra network (high-infra neighbors attract energy — current physics)
- Spent on: invest (→ infra), explore (seeding neighbors), and a base maintenance cost
- Doesn't diffuse freely on its own — it moves *because* of infra gradients

**Infra dynamics:**
- Gained by investing energy (invest action)
- Lost to maintenance: `infra *= (1 - decay_rate)` per step
- Can be liquidated back to energy (liquidate action)
- Creates the transport network that determines energy flow

### What this produces

With this three-layer model:

1. **Nutrient patches** create spatial heterogeneity that rewards exploration
2. **Contact digestion** creates foraging pressure — you must physically reach the patch
3. **Energy transport along infra** rewards building connected networks (not just local accumulation)
4. **Infra decay** makes structure costly to maintain — you need energy income to hold territory
5. **Patch depletion** creates boom-bust dynamics — a patch exploited by one organism eventually runs out, forcing expansion or collapse

This is much closer to what Physarum actually does.

## Transport Regime Question: Osmosis vs Gradients

The user asked whether energy movement should be slow (osmosis-like) or involve larger gradients.

The answer is probably **both, at different scales**:

- **Nutrient diffusion in the medium**: slow (osmosis-like). Nutrients spread ~1 cell/few hundred steps. Organisms deplete local nutrients faster than diffusion can replenish, creating steep local gradients.
- **Energy transport along organism infrastructure**: fast. This is active pumping, not diffusion. A slime mold can transport cytoplasm across centimeters in minutes. In the model this should be: energy moves along infra network at a rate proportional to infra strength.

The **combination** is what creates interesting behavior:
- Slow nutrient diffusion → organisms can't rely on food coming to them → must explore
- Fast internal transport → organisms that build connected networks can distribute energy efficiently → builds selection pressure for network architecture

## For the Coral Experiment Specifically

The coral experiment already has invest/liquidate and directional exploration. The physics issues are:

1. **Replace uniform source injection with patchwork nutrients**
   - Perlin/sinusoidal noise field for initial distribution
   - Slow diffusion (so patches persist and can be found)
   - Depletion by infra (contact digestion)
   - Slow regeneration

2. **Fix infra as transport, not sink**
   - Energy attracted to high-infra neighbors is fine *if* infra is spatially distributed
   - With nutrient patches, organisms will have infra concentrated at patches
   - Energy at a patch flows along the infra network → incentivizes connected networks

3. **Add infra maintenance cost**
   - `infra *= (1 - 0.001)` per step (already in known issues list)
   - Now holding territory costs ongoing energy income
   - Organisms that don't reach nutrients will slowly lose their infra

4. **Fix invest/liquidate activation** (softmax → tanh trade, already in known issues)

The order matters: fix (1) and (3) first — these change the selective landscape. Then let evolution run and see what strategies emerge. If organisms develop network-building behavior and foraging without designing for it, that's validation.

## Open Questions

- **Digestion timescale**: Should metabolism be instant (nutrient → energy in one step) or gradual (cells "process" nutrients over N steps)? Gradual would reward staying at a patch rather than moving on immediately.

- **Can infra block nutrient diffusion?** In a real biological system, dense tissue can impede diffusion. This would mean organisms partially "own" their nutrient supply by blocking it from spreading to neighbors.

- **Should energy leak across cells?** Currently energy flows based on infra gradient. Should there be a small baseline diffusion regardless of infra? This would represent the inevitable osmotic leakage in a biological membrane.

- **What's the right nutrient patch geometry?** Perlin noise gives smooth gradients. Discrete circular patches give sharp boundaries. The sharp-boundary case creates clearer "I found a patch" moments. Worth experimenting with both.

- **Source vs nutrient**: The current "source" channel could just be renamed "nutrient" and given proper diffusion + depletion dynamics. The existing energy_dynamics environments (patch, gradient, ring, rivers, volcano, random) already set up interesting nutrient configurations — they just need to behave physically instead of as static injection rates.

## Summary: The Core Reframe

**Current framing**: energy is a resource that appears everywhere and flows to concentration points.

**Better framing**: energy is the *metabolized* form of environmental nutrients. The nutrients are in the environment. The organism has to reach them. Its infrastructure is the network it builds to exploit and transport them.

This matches Physarum, matches bacterial biofilms, matches coral polyp networks. It's a more physical model, and it creates richer selective pressure because exploration and exploitation are now distinct strategies with real tradeoffs.

The invest/liquidate mechanism becomes: **convert raw energy into lasting structure (infra) that enables you to acquire more energy in the future**. This is the core of any growth strategy in biology — you spend now to earn more later.
