## What This Project Is

CoralAI is a research platform for studying how collective intelligence emerges from simple local rules under evolutionary pressure. The core bet: **if you get the physics right, interesting behavior emerges without designing for it.**

The design choices that make this distinctive:
- **No fitness function.** Selection is purely ecological — hold territory, attract energy, survive. The only reward is staying alive.
- **Continuous spatial evolution.** Mutation happens in-place (radiation events), not between generations. The population is always alive, always competing.
- **Physics-as-experiment.** The physics pipeline is the research variable. Different physics → different selective pressures → different evolved behaviors. You study the relationship between rules and outcomes, not the outcome of fixed rules.
- **NEAT + linear networks + Taichi GPU.** NEAT provides structural search; linear networks keep the per-cell forward pass cheap enough to run 160,000 cells simultaneously on a laptop GPU.

The central open question: *can organisms develop instrumental intelligence — navigation, resource exploitation, coordination — purely from survival pressure, without any designed signal?*

This is related to but distinct from: Lenia (beautiful but no evolution), Neural CA (growth dynamics but not ecological), POET (coevolving environments but episodic), open-ended evolution (same spirit, very different mechanism).

## Known Design Issues (not yet fixed)

These are understood problems, in priority order. The physics issues are in `experiments/coral/physics.py` which is now cleanly separated from the engine.

### 1. Softmax invest/liquidate (HIGH — affects evolution quality)
`softmax(dim=0)` on invest/liquidate forces `invest + liquidate = 1` per cell. Cells can never "do nothing." The network can only express direction (invest vs. liquidate), not intensity. Maximum waste at equilibrium.

**Fix:** replace with tanh signed trade channel in `physics.py`:
```python
trade = torch.tanh(raw_trade)
investments  = relu(trade)  * max_rate * energy
liquidations = relu(-trade) * max_rate * infra
```
One function, one `physics.py` edit, testable in 10 steps.

### 2. Culling is ecologically destructive (HIGH — affects biodiversity)
When genome count exceeds 100, the system kills the genomes with fewest cells. This purges rare genomes that may have just been seeded. Rare ≠ unfit.

**Fix:** pre-allocate a fixed-size genome pool (e.g., 256 slots) with a `free_indices` list. On genome extinction → slot returns to pool. No growth, no arbitrary killing. Pattern available in `origin/refactor`'s `Population` class.

### 3. No checkpointing (HIGH — affects experiment workflow)
`save_checkpoint()` in `SpaceEvolver` is a stub (`pass`). No run is recoverable. No longitudinal analysis is possible.

**Fix:** `torch.save(substrate.mem)` + `pickle.dump(genomes, ages)` into a timestamped `runs/` directory. The `checkpointer.py` (ported from `origin/analysis`) is ready to wire in. One function to fill in.

### 4. No spatial resource structure (MEDIUM — affects behavioral diversity)
Energy is injected uniformly everywhere. There are no patches to find, no gradients to follow. The optimal strategy is pure territorial defense; navigation provides no benefit.

**Fix:** Perlin noise resource patches on a slow-regenerating field. Cells that can find and exploit patches outcompete those that can't — creates navigation pressure.

### 5. Exploration is argmax + full energy (MEDIUM — reduces expressibility)
`argmax` throws away the direction weights. The cell can't invest partially in multiple directions. Colonization is all-or-nothing.

**Fix:** proportional bidding — each direction gets `activation * energy * max_fraction` of the available bid. Cells invest in multiple directions simultaneously.

### 6. No maintenance cost (LOW — reduces infrastructure value)
Infrastructure doesn't decay without energy input. A dead cell's infra persists indefinitely. This mutes the invest/liquidate tradeoff — infra is free to hold.

**Fix:** `infra *= (1 - decay_rate)` per step. Small constant (e.g., 0.001). Infra becomes a liability without energy, not just a free asset.

## Experiment Ladder (how to study this systematically)

The system currently has too many moving parts to attribute observed behavior to specific mechanisms. The right approach:

**Step 1 — Physics only:** Just `distribute_energy` and `distribute_infra`. No neural network. Do the physics self-organize into interesting spatial patterns?

**Step 2 — Add invest/liquidate with fixed networks:** With a non-evolving network, does the economy reach stable equilibria? What invest rates produce stable infra accumulation?

**Step 3 — Add exploration:** With fixed networks, does territorial competition produce meaningful patterns? Does infra-as-defense change qualitative behavior?

**Step 4 — Add evolution:** Now let NEAT evolve. What strategies emerge under each physics condition above?

**Step 5 — Add spatial resources and disturbance:** Does environmental heterogeneity drive more complex behavior? Does it increase or decrease biodiversity?

Currently the project runs at Step 4 without having characterized Steps 1–3. This makes it very hard to explain any observed behavior.

## Medium-Term Research Direction

Once checkpointing and logging exist, the experimental workflow becomes:

```
write physics variant → run 10k steps → analyze CSV + complexity metrics → compare
```

This is the core loop. The analysis tools already exist (ported from `origin/analysis`). The missing piece is the glue: checkpoint → analysis pipeline.

Specifically interesting experiments to run once the infrastructure is stable:

- **Softmax vs. tanh trade:** same everything, just change the invest/liquidate activation. Does the tanh version produce richer behavior? Higher complexity? More diverse genomes?
- **With vs. without spatial resources:** does adding Perlin patches change the complexity signature? Does it increase biodiversity?
- **Infra-as-defense vs. no defense:** does making infra defensive produce more stable territorial patterns?

These three experiments together would constitute a meaningful scientific contribution: a controlled study of how physics parameters affect the nature of evolved collective behavior.
