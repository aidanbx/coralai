## 1. The "Sessility" Problem: Is the Grid a Bug or a Feature?

You mentioned that everything being fixed (sessile) feels like a limitation. In Newtonian physics, yes. But in biology, **motion is often just a specific type of information transfer.**

* **Identity Transfer (The Wave):** In a grid, an "object" moves by shifting its state to the next cell. This is exactly how **Neural Cellular Automata (NCA)** work. A "particle" in your system isn't a coordinate; it's a **persistent pattern** of channel values that moves across the grid.
* **The Advantage:** Grid-based motion allows for **infinite density**. Particles in Newtonian systems can get "stuck" or require complex collision math. In your grid, "collisions" are just physics rules (e.g., if two patterns overlap, they merge or destroy each other).

## 2. Diverse Experiments Your Framework Could Run

If this framework becomes "Universal," here are the three distinct "Research Tracks" it would excel at:

### A. Evolutionary Game Theory & Multi-Level Selection

* **The "Cheater" Problem:** Can a colony of cooperative cells evolve an "immune system" to detect and kill "cancerous" cells (cells with mutations that take energy but don't contribute to infrastructure)?
* **Speciation Events:** If you introduce a physical barrier (a line of "hard" cells) that occasionally opens, can you observe **Allopatric Speciation**, where the two halves of the population evolve into incompatible species?

### B. Niche Construction & Ecological Engineering

* **Atmospheric Evolution:** Give the cells the ability to modify their environment (e.g., a "Oxygen" channel). If the cells "breathe" and change the channel values, they are forced to evolve to survive the very environment they created.
* **The "Great Oxidation" Experiment:** Can we recreate the event where life produced a byproduct so toxic (Oxygen) that it killed 99% of existing life, forcing the survivors to evolve aerobic respiration?

### C. Emergent Signaling & Language

* **Quorum Sensing:** Can cells evolve to "count" their neighbors using the `com` channels and change behavior only when a certain density is reached?
* **Spatial Signaling:** Can a "head" cell evolve to send a signal through the `com` channel to "tail" cells to coordinate a massive, directional migration?

## 4. The Potential Limitations

* **The "Channel Wall":** Since every cell has the same channels, adding a new "substance" or "organelle" requires adding a channel to the *entire world*. If you have 100 different experiments, your `Substrate` memory footprint could get bloated.
* **Topology:** Most of these are 2D Toroids. Some biological behaviors (like folding) require 3D or non-Euclidean geometry.
* **Action Space Explosion:** As you add more channels, the "Brain" (the NEAT network) has more inputs and outputs. The "search space" for evolution might become too vast, leading to millions of steps of "nothing" before a useful mutation occurs.
