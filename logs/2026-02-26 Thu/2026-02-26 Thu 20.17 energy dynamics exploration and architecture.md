*CoralAI design analysis — 2026-02-26*

## 1. Does Energy Actually Move? Can It Be Found?

**Yes, energy moves — but no, it cannot be found.**

Two mechanisms move energy spatially:

**`flow_energy_up` (in `energy_physics`)**: At every step, each cell distributes its energy to neighbors in proportion to their infrastructure:

```python
out_energy[neigh] += central_energy * (neigh_infra / sum_of_neighbor_infra)
```

A cell with high infra draws energy toward itself from all neighbors — including cells owned by different genomes. This is passive and automatic. A cell cannot prevent its energy from being drawn toward a high-infra neighbor. This is significant: **building infrastructure is a form of resource extraction from neighbors**, not just personal storage.

**`explore_physics`**: When a cell colonizes a neighbor, the current code does:

```python
bid = mem[0, inds.energy, neigh_x, neigh_y]   # full energy of the explorer
energy_delta[neigh_x, neigh_y] -= bid
bid = 0.9                                        # cost: only 0.9 arrives
infra_delta[i, j] += bid
```

The exploring cell *spends all its energy* to place 0.9 infra in the target cell and take it over. So exploration *converts energy to infra in a new location* — it's colonization with a 10% friction cost. Note: the exploring cell's energy is spent, so a successful colonization leaves the new cell with 0 energy but 0.9 infra.

**What "finding energy" would mean**: Currently there are no spatially-structured energy sources. Energy is injected uniformly as:

```python
energy += (randn + energy_offset) * 0.1
infra  += (randn + energy_offset) * 0.1
```

The `energy_offset` is sinusoidal (period 50 steps), producing "warm" and "cold" periods. It's spatially homogeneous — there are no hotspots, no patches to discover. This is a major gap relative to EINCASM which had `port` sites distributed by Lévy flight. **Spatial resource heterogeneity is probably the single most impactful missing feature for driving interesting evolved behavior.**

## 2. Multi-Directional Exploration

### The Current Problem

The current explore pipeline:

```python
max_act_i = torch.argmax(substrate.mem[0, inds.acts_explore], dim=0)
```

This selects *one* direction per cell and commits all energy to it. The explore activation magnitudes (after softmax) are discarded — only argmax is used.

Consequences:
- A cell that wants to expand in three directions equally must pick one arbitrarily
- The multi-directional information encoded by the network is thrown away
- Colonization is binary and instantaneous — either a cell is taken over or it isn't
- A cell with 50/50 preference between two directions flips erratically based on floating point

### Alternative: Proportional Bidding

Instead of argmax + full energy, each cell bids *proportionally* to its activation in each direction, spending a fraction of its energy per direction:

```python
# Each direction gets a proportional fraction of available energy
bid_per_dir = explore_activation * energy * max_bid_fraction   # shape [4, W, H]
# For each target cell, find the highest incoming bid (still winner-takes-all on ownership)
# But the cost is proportional, not all-or-nothing
```

This lets cells genuinely invest in multiple directions simultaneously at different intensities. The cell doesn't go bankrupt on a single direction.

### Alternative: Gradient Colonization

Rather than binary genome takeover, colonization could be *probabilistic* or *gradual*:

- A strong bid gives high *probability* of takeover, not certainty
- A cell could hold a *mixture* of genome influences (relevant to symbiosis — see below)
- Infra in the target cell contributes a "defense" bonus to the incumbent genome

```python
# Example: takeover probability decreases with defender's infra
defense = target_infra / (target_infra + bid_energy)
takeover_prob = 1 - defense
```

This would make well-established colonies more stable and give cells a concrete reason to build infrastructure beyond just energy attraction.

## 3. Infrastructure as Defense — And the Immune System Question

### The Current Model Has No Defense

Under current explore physics, any cell can be taken over regardless of how much infra it has. Infra provides no protection — it just attracts energy and enables `flow_energy_up`. This means a newly-seeded organism with zero infra can colonize a well-established cell equally well if it bids more energy.

### Infra as a Defensive Moat

The most natural fix: infra raises the "cost to colonize."

```python
effective_bid = incoming_bid - target_infra * defense_coefficient
# Only colonize if effective_bid > 0
```

Now cells have a reason to build infrastructure not just for energy attraction but for *territorial defense*. This makes the invest/liquidate tradeoff genuinely strategic: invest = grow, fortify; liquidate = mobilize energy for exploring.

### Immune Systems and Self/Non-Self Recognition

The current model is purely adversarial between genomes — all foreign cells are treated identically. There's no concept of "self" recognition or tolerance.

A minimal self/non-self mechanism:
- Cells broadcast their genome ID on a `com` channel
- A cell receiving a colonization bid from the *same genome* boosts it (kin selection)
- A cell receiving a bid from a *different genome* reduces it (immune response proportional to com signal mismatch)

This would create the preconditions for **kin-cooperative behavior** — cells of the same genome assist each other's expansion rather than competing purely on energy.

### Symbiosis as Default (Lewis Thomas)

Lewis Thomas's key insight in *The Lives of a Cell*: most organisms are deeply symbiotic, and immunity (the ability to distinguish and reject foreign biology) is a *late evolutionary development*. The default biological state is cooperation and cohabitation, not war.

The current model has this backwards: every foreign genome is automatically an enemy. There's no mechanism for mutualism, no benefit to coexisting with a different genome.

A more natural model:
- Different genomes can occupy the same cell simultaneously (mixed occupancy)
- Genomes sharing a cell both contribute to and draw from that cell's energy/infra
- Conflict arises when resource draws exceed supply — *competition emerges from scarcity, not from the rules*
- Genomes that stably coexist could even specialize (one genome optimizes energy attraction, another optimizes exploration)

This would require a richer cell state (multiple genome presences weighted by abundance), which is more complex, but would produce much richer ecological dynamics.

## 4. Energy vs. Infrastructure: What's Really the Difference?

This is worth questioning directly.

**Formal difference in the current model:**
- Energy: drawn toward high-infra cells (`flow_energy_up`), spills when over `max_energy`, injected by noise
- Infra: spills when over `max_infra`, built from energy via invest, consumed by exploration bids, slowly distributed spatially

**What they share**: Both are bounded, both spread to neighbors when over threshold, both conserved within `invest_liquidate`.

**The actual metabolic analogy:**
- Energy ≈ free energy / ATP / metabolic currency — *liquid, available now, flows easily*
- Infra ≈ structural biomass / organelles / cell wall — *fixed investment, persists, provides capacity*

The key asymmetry is that infra *attracts* energy but not vice versa. This is directional: a high-infra cell is like a capacitor that charges itself from its environment. A high-energy cell without infra is unstable — it spills energy and has no mechanism to hold it.

**Is total (energy + infra) the real cell capacity?** Within `invest_liquidate`, yes — the total is conserved. But the dynamics treat them very differently. A cell with energy=100, infra=0 is unstable (will spill energy everywhere) and can't colonize efficiently. A cell with energy=0, infra=100 is stable but starving for the energy that will flow toward it. The two-part representation creates a genuine strategic space: liquid vs. fixed assets.

**A simpler model worth considering**: One channel `biomass`, one channel `state ∈ [0,1]` indicating liquid/structural fraction. The tradeoff between liquid and structural is still there but the total capacity is explicitly bounded as one thing. This is closer to how actual cells work — total dry mass is the constraint, and cells regulate the proportion of energy storage vs structure.

## 5. Energy Flow: A Full System View

Tracing energy through the system end-to-end:

```
SOURCES
  ├── noise injection: (randn + energy_offset) * 0.1  [homogeneous, sinusoidal amplitude]
  └── [MISSING] spatially structured resource patches

WITHIN-CELL DYNAMICS
  ├── invest: energy → infra  (controlled by network)
  └── liquidate: infra → energy  (controlled by network)
  (conserved: total energy+infra per cell unchanged by this step)

BETWEEN-CELL FLOWS
  ├── flow_energy_up: energy drifts toward high-infra neighbors  (passive, automatic)
  ├── distribute_energy: excess energy spills equally to all neighbors
  ├── distribute_infra: excess infra spills equally to all neighbors
  └── explore: cell pays energy to colonize neighbor (10% friction loss)

SINKS
  ├── death: cells below (energy+infra) < 0.05 → genome=-1
  ├── explore friction: 10% of colonization bid is destroyed
  ├── clamp at 100: energy/infra cannot exceed 100 (soft ceiling)
  └── random chunk kill (every 50 steps, radius 5): destroys wealth in killed area

MISSING
  ├── maintenance cost: infrastructure should decay without energy input
  ├── metabolic cost: running the neural network should cost energy
  └── spatial resource discovery
```

The most important gap: **there is no spatial structure to energy sources.** All cells receive the same expected energy injection. This means there's nothing to *navigate toward* — the optimal strategy is purely about holding territory and managing the energy/infra ratio, not about finding and exploiting resources. Adding spatially-varying resource patches (as EINCASM had) would immediately create navigation pressure.

## 6. The Softmax Problem — Summary of Fixes

See `invest_liquidate_analysis.md` for full analysis. Quick summary:

**Current problem**: `softmax(dim=0)` on invest/liquidate forces `invest + liquidate = 1` at each cell, meaning cells always simultaneously invest and liquidate. No "do nothing" state, magnitude lost, maximum waste at equilibrium.

**Recommended fix** — single signed trade channel:

```python
trade = torch.tanh(raw_trade)          # ∈ (-1, 1)
max_rate = 0.3
investments  = relu(trade)  * max_rate * energy
liquidations = relu(-trade) * max_rate * infra
```

Positive = anabolic (build up), negative = catabolic (break down), zero = rest. Maps directly to cellular metabolism: mTOR-active (fed, building) vs. autophagy-active (fasted, recycling). A cell that just received a lot of energy would drive trade positive; a cell low on energy would drive it negative to mobilize reserves.

## 7. The Culling Problem

**Current behavior**: When genomes exceed 100, the system kills the lowest cell-count genomes. Cells owned by dead genomes become unowned (-1).

**Why this is bad**:
- Purges rare genomes that may have just been seeded and haven't expanded yet
- Penalizes legitimate specialization (a genome that occupies few cells but is very fit)
- Creates memory management cliffs — sudden mass die-offs
- The threshold of 100 is arbitrary

**Root cause**: The weight tensor grows as `[n_genomes, 1, n_acts, n_inputs]`. On a 400×400 grid with pop_size=50 and radiation every 50 steps, genome count grows indefinitely without culling.

**Better solutions**:

1. **Natural extinction only**: Remove a genome from the registry only when it has had 0 cells for N consecutive steps. No artificial killing. The genome count ceiling becomes determined by the ecological carrying capacity, not a hard limit.

2. **Fixed-size ring buffer**: Maintain a fixed-size array of genome slots (e.g., 256). When creating a new genome, reuse the slot of the genome with the fewest cells (accepting that very rare genomes get overwritten). Avoids unbounded memory growth without arbitrary killing.

3. **Compact on demand**: When memory pressure hits, do a single compaction pass — remove genomes with 0 cells (truly extinct), remap IDs. This is the existing `reduce_population_to_threshold` logic but triggered only on true extinction, not on population size.

4. **Genome sharing/inheritance**: Instead of creating a new genome slot for each mutation, allow the same weight matrix to be shared by nearby cells. Mutations produce small perturbations in a continuous weight space rather than discrete new entries.

## 8. Is the Current Implementation Too Complex to Study?

Yes. The current system has at least 6 simultaneous interacting mechanisms:

1. Neural network forward pass (NEAT-evolved weights)
2. Invest/liquidate economy
3. Explore colonization
4. Energy/infra diffusion physics
5. Sinusoidal weather (energy_offset)
6. Random chunk killing

It's very hard to attribute any observed behavior to a specific mechanism. A natural experimental progression would be:

**Step 1 — Minimal physics only**: Just `distribute_energy` and `distribute_infra`. No neural network. Do energy and infra self-organize into interesting spatial patterns? This tests the physics in isolation.

**Step 2 — Add invest/liquidate**: With fixed (non-evolved) networks, does the economy produce stable equilibria? What invest rates lead to stable infra accumulation?

**Step 3 — Add exploration**: With fixed networks, does territorial competition produce meaningful patterns? What's the effect of infra-as-defense vs. no defense?

**Step 4 — Add evolution**: Now let NEAT evolve. What strategies emerge under each of the above conditions?

**Step 5 — Add weather/disturbance**: Does environmental variation drive more complex evolved behavior? Does it increase or decrease biodiversity?

This is the experimental ladder the project needs to climb. Right now it's running at Step 4-5 without having characterized Steps 1-3.
